Genre with the largest vocabulary

A comparison of lyrical vocabularies of representative artists of popular music genres

By Varun Jewalikar and Federica Fragapane at Musixmatch

A few months ago, here at Musixmatch, we conducted a study titled ‘The largest vocabulary in music’ (we suggest you to give it a quick read). We studied the vocabularies of the 99 top selling musicians. A small section of that study was dedicated to comparing vocabularies across genres but this wasn’t a fair comparison. Mainly because the 99 top selling musicians split by genre aren’t necessarily the representative artists for a given genre and the number of artists considered in various genres is unequal so the analysis was skewed.

So we decided to dig deeper into comparing the vocabulary across various popular genres.

Ideally, we would want a crowdsourced list (musicologists/avid listeners voting for which artists are representative of each genre) of artists for each genre. This would be quite a task in itself. So instead we consider the list of most searched artists in each genre as the representative artists. These lists can be easily retrieved from google as shown below

Looking at the genres and their representative artists, maybe you can already wager a bet on which genres/artists might come out on the top but there are a lot of surprises in store.

Disparity in catalogue sizes of various artists

Some artists are very prolific (releasing more than an album every year) while some only release a few albums during their whole career. If we directly compare the vocabulary sizes of these artists the analysis will be skewed in favour of the more prolific artists. Instead we use the following definition at the

Vocabulary Size is the number of unique words used by an artist across the first 10000 words(or less) written by them.

Generally this covers around 3-4 albums and is a good representation of the writing style of an artist.

Comparison of genres





No points for guessing

It should be no surprise that hiphop is the genre with the highest average vocabulary. In our previous study, hiphop artists had dominated the overall vocabulary rankings. The lyrics are dense and lots of slang is used which gives rappers a big lead over other wordsmiths. Also, Hiphop leads the other genres by a healthy margin (it leads the next genre by 430 words on average).

Surprise surprise

We expected a more poetry driven genre like Folk or Country to take the second place but were pleasantly surprised to find that it is actually Heavy Metal. A simple guess for this could be the myriad of complicated concepts heavy metal lyrics try to address which calls for using a lot of new words. Also Heavy Metal leads the following genres by a healthy margin of 70 words on average.

The runners up

Folk and Rock come in at #3 and #4 respectively. The difference in the average vocabulary sizes for these two is around 31 words. They are followed closely by Country and Indie Rock at #5 and #6. The average vocabulary these four genres is x words. It defines the ‘plateau’ region of this study.

Last but not the least

Pop has the least average vocabulary of all the genres. But generally pop artists have a wider reach (across barriers of language and country) as compared to the other genres. One could guess that the simplicity of the lyrics would be one of the defining features of a pop song.

Conclusion

It goes without saying that vocabulary size is just a number and it can’t quantify the simplicity, complexity or profoundness of what a song conveys. As previously stated, it is just a fun metric to look at our favourite genres and artists in a new light.
